About

About

I'm a robotics master's student at Georgia Tech working on the question of how autonomous systems can guarantee safety when their dynamics are learned from data rather than derived from first principles. My current research builds data-driven control barrier functions for multi-agent collision avoidance, validated in simulation and on real quadrotor hardware.

My background is in physics, where I did morphological fitting of galaxy light profiles — different domain, same discipline: build the model, quantify the uncertainty, and don't trust a result you haven't validated. I bring that standard to control systems work, with a strong preference for getting things running on hardware over leaving them in simulation.

I'm targeting roles in autonomous vehicles, the space industry, and large-scale robotics — places where control theory meets deployed systems.

Research

Data-Driven Control Barrier Functions for Multi-Agent Collision Avoidance

2024 — Present

C3U Lab (Control, Coordination, and Competition under Uncertainty) · Georgia Tech · Advisor: Dr. Sarah Li

  • Formulated direct data-driven LQR controllers as semidefinite programs (De Persis & Tesi formulation) and implemented the synthesis pipeline in CVXPY, bypassing explicit system identification.
  • Validated learned controllers on quadrotor dynamics in AirSim before transferring to hardware.
  • Deployed and tested on Crazyflie 2.1 quadrotors in a Vicon motion-capture environment, closing the loop from convex program to real-time flight.
  • Extending the framework toward control barrier function constraints for provable inter-agent safety guarantees in multi-agent settings.

Multi-Component Morphological Fitting of Early-Type Galaxies

2023 — 2024

Undergraduate Thesis · BS Physics

  • Refined effective-radius measurements for a sample of early-type galaxies from the Atlas3D survey using GALFIT multi-component light-profile fits.
  • Demonstrated that multi-component decompositions yield significantly more accurate structural parameters than the single-component fits used in the original study.
